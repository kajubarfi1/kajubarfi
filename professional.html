Texas Instruments Summer 2025 · 12 Weeks
Technical Product Marketing Engineering Intern · Low Power Connectivity Solutions

If Valero taught me what it means to be in the field, TI taught me what it means to tell a story. I spent the summer as a Product Marketing Engineer in TI's Low Power Connectivity Solutions group, and my entire project revolved around one thing: Channel Sounding — a brand new feature in the Bluetooth 6.0 specification.

I was given a technology and full creative liberty to figure out how TI should talk about it. That meant starting from scratch — understanding the RF fundamentals behind Channel Sounding, researching how competitors were positioning their solutions, and then building out a marketing strategy to fill the gaps I found.

By the end of the summer, I'd put together a competitive analysis comparing TI's Channel Sounding collateral against competitor companies and identifying where TI's messaging was falling short. From there, I restructured existing marketing materials to address those gaps, wrote a 10-page technical white paper highlighting real-world use cases, filmed a Channel Sounding informational video, and created a demo video showing TI's CC27xx solution in action. All of which will soon be live on ti.com.

The coolest part was realizing that marketing engineering isn't just "making things look nice" — it's about taking something deeply technical and translating it into a story that makes a customer care. It's a skill I didn't expect to fall in love with.

Being able to take a very complex problem and explain it in terms that a customer can understand — that's what separates a good PME from a great one.

☕ my takeaways
  1. Be proactive with feedback. A huge part of marketing is catering your solution to a wide range of people. Taking insights from others — no matter how small — makes your strategy more versatile. I learned to actively seek out opinions instead of waiting for them.
  2. Break abstract concepts into digestible pieces. Expecting to understand everything at first glance isn't reasonable, especially with complex RF technologies like Channel Sounding. The ability to simplify without losing accuracy is everything in this role.
  3. Be adaptable. The market is constantly shifting, which means what works today might not work tomorrow. Understanding the "why" behind your work makes it so much easier to pivot when things change — and they always do.
Valero Energy Corporation Summer 2024 · 12 Weeks
Electrical Engineering Intern

This was my first real taste of power engineering, and it completely changed how I think about electrical systems. I spent 12 weeks at a Valero refinery working alongside a team of electrical engineers who were responsible for keeping everything running — and I mean everything.

Most of my days were split between the field and the office. In the field, I was getting hands-on with high voltage systems up to 12.5 kV — working around switchgears, motor control centers, and learning how three-phase power keeps a refinery alive. In the office, I was tracing through wiring diagrams, writing up Management of Change documents, and helping develop technical drawings for large-scale projects.

The project I'm most proud of was owning a motor protection relay replacement from start to finish. I redlined the wiring diagrams, programmed the SEL 849 relays, and was on-site for every step of the installation. There's something about seeing a project go from paper to reality that just hits different.

I also supported the installation of a voltage ridethrough system for a critical motor — one of those things where you realize how much careful engineering goes into keeping a single piece of equipment from going down.

What surprised me most was how much of this job is communication. The technical work is only half of it — the other half is planning, coordination, and adapting when things inevitably don't go according to plan.

☕ my takeaways
  1. Show up to everything. Even if a meeting has nothing to do with your work, understanding how the rest of the company operates makes you way better at your own job. Some of my best learning moments came from presentations I almost skipped.
  2. Ask the big-picture questions first. When you're lost (and you will be), don't try to understand every detail right away. Ask questions that help you see the overall process — the specifics start making sense once you have the full picture.
  3. Write everything down. Seriously. Every meeting, every field visit, every random thing someone explains to you. Future you will thank present you.
